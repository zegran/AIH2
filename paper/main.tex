\begin{abstract}
\noindent The aluminum--water hydrolysis literature for hydrogen production reports
parameter effects that contradict one another across studies---most visibly a
direction-changing particle-size effect and activation energies spanning
$3.5$--$102.6$\,kJ\,mol$^{-1}$---which blocks data-driven design and leaves open whether
the contradictions reflect genuine physico-chemical regimes or artifacts of isolated,
non-standardized experimental conditions. We unify the scattered literature into a
provenance-tracked open dataset (315 condition-level yield rows from 31 studies, plus a
separate 76-row kinetic table) and analyze it with leakage-controlled evaluation, a
study-level variance decomposition, and a within-study consistency test, with
pre-registered claims and transparent nulls. Under study-grouped cross-validation the
strong apparent predictability of random splits collapses (optimism gap $0.62$--$0.85$
in $R^2$), and a between-study variance decomposition shows that \emph{methodological}
covariates are associated with $\approx 55\%$ of the between-study yield variance versus
$\approx 2\%$ for the physico-chemical regime label (\texttt{system\_class}), with the
temperature-control method alone the single largest source ($\approx 33\%$). The flagship
particle-size ``contradiction'' is internally consistent within every study that varies
size ($5/5$ negative), identifying it as a cross-study integration artifact rather than a
physical sign reversal. We therefore conclude---associationally and at $n\!\approx\!31$
studies---that the literature's apparent contradictions are predominantly methodological;
we release the curated dataset, the reproducible analysis, and a minimum-information
reporting standard for aluminum-hydrolysis studies.
\end{abstract}
