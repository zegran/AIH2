\section{Results}

\subsection{TEST~1 negative control: the cross-study signal is not a genuine
replication signal}
\label{sec:test1}

Before reporting the primary results, we establish that the apparent within-regime
cross-study signal is not genuine. Within the largest regime (\texttt{pure\_al\_alkali},
$n\!=\!162$, 9~studies), a rows-weighted LOSO skill of $+0.825$ looks impressive.
However, one study, \citeauthor{porciuncula2012}~\cite{porciuncula2012}, contributes
120~of 162~rows (74\%) and achieves LOSO skill $+0.926$; when skill is averaged uniformly
over studies (study-macro LOSO) rather than over rows, it falls to $-0.311$. MAE doubles
from $9.3$ to $17.2$\,\% yield on removing that study from the training pool.
Of the nine studies, 5/9 beat the class mean when tested individually; the four failing
studies miss badly (skill $-0.10$ to $-3.3$). The row-weighted and study-macro metrics
diverge by $1.14$: the headline score is porciuncula-weighted, not a replication signal.
A row-weighted metric should therefore not be used to claim cross-study performance on
this corpus.

\subsection{The literature does not pool: a large and robust optimism gap}

Table~\ref{tab:cv_summary} reports the full per-model cross-validation results. Under
random $k$-fold splits, tree models reach $R^2\!\approx\!0.55$--$0.57$ (random forest
$0.564$, LightGBM $0.572$, XGBoost $0.545$). Under study-grouped cross-validation the
same models collapse to $R^2\!<\!0$ (all six regressors perform worse than predicting
the global mean) for optimism gaps of $0.62$--$0.85$ (tree models) and up to $6.27$
(ElasticNet). The gap persists after removing \citeauthor{porciuncula2012}: RF gap
$0.622\to0.484$, LGBM $0.828\to0.730$, XGB $0.845\to0.632$, confirming it is not driven
by one dominant study (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap}).

\subsection{Methodology, not physical regime, explains the between-study variance}
\label{sec:variance_results}

At the study level, $\approx\!50\%$ of the total yield variance lies between studies
($\eta^2_{\text{study}}\!=\!0.496$; this is a robust one-way decomposition, as the
null-model random-intercept in the mixed-effects fit collapsed to the boundary). The
physico-chemical regime label explains $R^2_{\texttt{system\_class}}\!=\!0.018$
(cluster-bootstrap 95\,\% CI $[0.015,\,0.373]$); the point estimate is low, but the
wide interval (upper bound $\approx\!37\,\%$) reflects the limited corpus ($n\!=\!31$
studies) and does not exclude moderate regime effects.
Methodological covariates jointly explain $R^2\!=\!0.717$ (CI $[0.443,\,1.000]$),
$\approx\!40\times$ more (Fig.~\ref{fig:variance}). The single largest individual source is the
\emph{hydrogen measurement apparatus}: $R^2_{\texttt{measurement\_method}}\!=\!0.425$
(CI $[0.024,\,0.806]$; associational; $n\!=\!25$ studies reporting apparatus details;
6~studies have no apparatus metadata). Temperature-control method explains
$R^2_{\texttt{temperature\_control}}\!=\!0.081$ (CI $[0.002,\,0.240]$);
studies using a self-heating protocol report higher mean yields
($86\%$) than isothermal-bath studies ($72\%$) across $7$ and $24$ studies respectively.
These two levels exhaust the temperature-control variable in the corrected dataset
(Section~\ref{sec:qc}; two studies originally coded as \texttt{uncontrolled} were
reclassified to \texttt{isothermal\_bath} during inter-rater adjudication, see Table~\ref{tab:qc_summary}).

The measurement-method signal is not a relabeling of study identity or physical regime.
The dominant levels (\texttt{water\_displacement}, $n\!=\!19$ studies;
\texttt{other}, $n\!=\!1$; \texttt{pressure}, $n\!=\!3$; \texttt{mass\_flow}, $n\!=\!1$;
\texttt{gc}, $n\!=\!1$) each span multiple regimes. The measurement-method contribution
\emph{survives controlling for regime}: incremental variance explained by measurement method
given \texttt{system\_class} is $+0.512$ (bootstrap CI $[+0.069,\,+0.813]$,
excluding $0$), whereas regime given method adds only $+0.044$. Temperature control also
contributes beyond regime: incremental $+0.107$ (CI $[+0.009,\,+0.316]$, excluding $0$).
Residual between-study spread within measurement-apparatus groups remains substantial,
so these predictors identify the dominant sources of between-study variance, not all of it.

\subsection{Mixed-effects regime moderation: not supported (H2)}

A mixed-effects meta-regression ($n\!=\!299$ rows, $27$~studies, REML, random intercept
per study) with \texttt{system\_class} as the fixed regime effect finds no significant
moderation at any pre-registered Holm-corrected bar. The joint Wald test on the
\texttt{system\_class} main effect gives $p\!=\!0.32$ on the full dataset,
$p\!=\!0.45$ removing \citeauthor{porciuncula2012}, and $p\!=\!0.41$ on A/B-tier
studies only. None of the $8$~parameter$\times$\texttt{system\_class} interactions
survive Holm correction in any of the three subsets ($0/8$ every time; closest:
temperature$\times$liquid\_metal in A/B-tier, raw $p\!=\!0.007$, Holm $p\!=\!0.057$).
A cluster bootstrap over studies confirms fragility: the system\_class main effect
reaches $p\!<\!0.05$ in only $34\%$ of $200$ bootstrap resamples (median bootstrap
$p\!=\!0.14$). H2 is \textbf{not supported}: conditioning on physico-chemical regime
does not resolve the between-study contradictions.

\subsection{Activation energy does not organize by regime (H3)}

Per-study median apparent activation energies from the 17-study kinetic sample are
$52.0$\,kJ\,mol$^{-1}$ (\texttt{pure\_al\_alkali}, $n\!=\!5$), $47.4$
(\texttt{waste\_al}, $n\!=\!3$), $36.2$ (\texttt{liquid\_metal\_activated}, $n\!=\!4$;
range $8.5$--$58.0$), $39.2$ (\texttt{al\_alloy}, $n\!=\!1$), and $23.7$
(\texttt{mechanically\_activated}, $n\!=\!4$) (Table~\ref{tab:ea_summary};
Fig.~\ref{fig:ea}). A variance decomposition of per-study $E_\mathrm{a}$ by regime
gives $\eta^2\!=\!0.361$, permutation $p\!=\!0.129$ (bootstrap 95\,\% CI $[0.127,\,0.808]$)
on the four classes with $\ge\!3$ studies. H3 is \textbf{not supported}: the
coarse regime label does not explain the apparent $E_\mathrm{a}$ spread. The
\texttt{liquid\_metal\_activated} within-class spread ($8.5$--$58.0$\,kJ\,mol$^{-1}$)
is as large as the between-class spread, suggesting unresolved within-regime
heterogeneity rather than true thermodynamic regime structure.

\subsection{The flagship particle-size contradiction is a cross-study artifact}

The literature's headline contradiction is a direction-changing particle-size effect.
Within every study that varies particle size ($5/5$), the within-study effect has the
\emph{same} sign: smaller particles give higher yield (Spearman $\rho\!=\!-0.29$
to $-0.87$; Fig.~\ref{fig:particle}). The apparent ``flip'' across the literature
arises from comparing different size ranges and material regimes between studies, not
from any within-study sign reversal. The flagship contradiction is an integration
artifact. Coverage note: particle size ($d_{50}$) is recorded for only $19\,\%$ of
yield rows ($59/315$), and two regimes (\texttt{al\_alloy} and
\texttt{mechanically\_activated}) have zero particle-size entries; the $5/5$ finding
is limited to the regimes and study designs where particle size was reported as a
variable.

\subsection{Within-study predictability is heterogeneous (a transparent null)}

Whether each study is internally predictable varies widely. Within-study LOSO skill is
clearly positive for well-designed parameter sweeps with a smooth response surface
(Fig.~\ref{fig:surface}; \citeauthor{qiao2019}~\cite{qiao2019} $+0.84$,
\citeauthor{zhang2024}~\cite{zhang2024} $+0.86$). But the median skill across 14~testable
studies is $-0.145$ (cluster-bootstrap 95\,\% CI $[-0.447,\,+0.233]$), with $5/14$
positive. The between-study variance ($\eta^2_{\text{study}}\!=\!0.496$) and the consistent
particle-size direction both indicate that the underlying physics is not inconsistent;
the low within-study skill reflects heterogeneous experimental designs and per-study
sample sizes of $5$--$120$ rows rather than genuine within-study physical noise.

\subsection{Permutation importance: methodology features dominate}

In-sample random-forest permutation importance ranks \texttt{particle\_size} ($0.597$)
first, \texttt{in\_wt\_pct} ($0.488$) second, and \texttt{temperature\_k} ($0.269$) third.
The regime label \texttt{system\_class} ranks tenth ($0.032$), one-nineteenth of
\texttt{particle\_size}, consistent with the variance-decomposition finding that
methodology rather than regime accounts for apparent yield differences. Alkali concentration
ranks fourteenth ($0.009$). SHAP cross-fold rank stability (mean pairwise Spearman across
grouped folds) is $0.66$\,/\,$0.68$\,/\,$0.70$ (seeds $0$\,/\,$42$\,/\,$7$): the feature
hierarchy is moderately stable but should not be over-interpreted given the poor
between-study predictive $R^2$.

\subsection{Robustness sensitivities}

Two pre-specified sensitivity checks were run to assess how robust the headline findings
are to study-quality cut and particle-size reporting.

\textbf{OA-subset (tier A+B only, $n\!=\!19$).}
Restricting to the 19 studies rated tier~A or~B eliminates all 12 tier-C studies.
After this restriction:
$R^2_{\texttt{system\_class}}\!=\!0.094$ (still low);
$R^2_{\texttt{measurement\_method}}\!=\!0.073$ (drops substantially);
$R^2_{\texttt{temperature\_control}}\!=\!0.065$ (also drops).
All individual predictors weaken, which is expected when the tier-C studies (which
include the lowest-yield and self-heating records) are removed, reducing between-study
spread. The headline direction (regime $<$ methodological predictors) holds, but the
absolute magnitudes are sensitive to quality-tier inclusion; this is a legitimate
limitation of corpus size and tier composition.

\textbf{Particle-size reporting sensitivity.}
Among studies that report particle size as an experimental variable ($n\!=\!5$ of $31$;
$59/315$ rows), a Fisher's exact test comparing studies that do vs.\ do not report
particle size finds no significant differences in quality-tier distribution
($p\!=\!0.35$) or temperature-control composition ($p\!=\!0.61$), supporting the
generalisability of the within-study particle-size finding to the broader corpus.
