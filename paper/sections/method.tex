\section{Data and methods}

\subsection{Literature screening and dataset construction}

We identified candidate studies by keyword search, forward and backward citation
chaining from established reviews, and systematic inspection of relevant journal indices
through early 2026. Full texts were obtained for all candidate articles; 59 papers were
screened against the inclusion criterion: at least one numeric hydrogen outcome (yield or
reaction rate) reported with the experimental conditions explicitly stated. Thirty-one
studies met that criterion and constitute the data corpus
\cite{wen2018,prabu2021,wanghq2017,yavor2013,rin2021,trowell2022,deng2010,porciuncula2012,zhang2024,liuyh2017,qiao2019,meng2022,xiao2018,guan2019,preez2018,chen2020,xiao2020,davies2022mat,ilyukhina2012,dudoladov2016,fischman2020,lu2017,jayaraman2015,gupta2025,ho2016,knoks2025,mezulis2023,martinezv2026,buryakov2023met,urbonav2024,fadhilah2023};
the remaining screened papers (comparative reviews and system-specific studies that do
not report extractable condition-level yield or rate data) are cited in Related Work by
regime (Section~\ref{sec:related})
\cite{razavi2016,yang2018,shmelev2016,noland2020,wangxy2021,elitzur2014,tan2016,godart2019,razavi2013,preez2019,david2012,slocum2020}.

From these 31 studies we extracted every distinct experimental condition, yielding a
flat-format dataset with 30~columns per row. Physical parameters include temperature (K),
alkali-solution concentration (mol\,L$^{-1}$), particle size (median $d_{50}$, $\mu$m),
aluminium purity or alloy composition, activator type and loading, and
water-to-aluminium mass ratio. Methodological metadata encode temperature-control method
(\texttt{isothermal\_bath}, \texttt{self\_heating}; see Section~\ref{sec:qc} for
corrections applied during inter-rater adjudication), measurement
method (\texttt{water\_displacement}, \texttt{pressure}, \texttt{gc}, \texttt{mass\_flow},
\texttt{other}), vessel geometry
class, water type, and quality tier (A/B/C). Full provenance is stored per row: study
DOI, the table or figure from which each value was read, extraction method (direct or
digitized), and extractor identity. The grouping key for all cross-study analyses is the
study DOI. Absent quantities (conditions where $\eta\!=\!0$ is physically meaningful) are
encoded as $0$; unreported quantities are stored as \texttt{NaN} and never conflated
with absent ones.

Each study is assigned exactly one physico-chemical regime label
$\texttt{system\_class} \in \{\texttt{pure\_al\_alkali},\;\texttt{al\_alloy},\;
\texttt{mechanically\_activated},\;\texttt{liquid\_metal\_activated},\;\texttt{waste\_al}\}$,
derived from the source materials-and-methods section and verified during the
classification audit described below. The final yield dataset has 315 condition-level
rows from 31 studies (Fig.~\ref{fig:dataset}); a separate 76-row kinetic table
holds Arrhenius activation energies and reaction rates, with their own provenance and
fit-quality columns, and is never merged into the yield table.
The tables remain separate because yield ($\eta$, a cumulative fraction at a fixed endpoint)
and kinetic parameters ($E_\mathrm{a}$, peak rate, half-time) are distinct outcome types
with incompatible units and different quality gates; merging would require normalization
assumptions not justified in a meta-analytic context.
Table~\ref{tab:dataset_composition}
summarises the per-regime composition; the complete 31-study source list with
temperature, concentration, and particle-size ranges appears in Table~SI-1.

\subsection{Quality control}
\label{sec:qc}

Quality control followed five procedures, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:qc_summary}.
First, approximately 16\,\% of yield rows (52~rows from 5~studies) were re-extracted
independently using different source annotations; 52/52 rows reached exact numeric
agreement, confirming extraction fidelity. Second, a classification audit
re-derived \texttt{system\_class} for all 31 studies directly from source materials and
methods: one error was found and corrected. \citeauthor{dudoladov2016}
\cite{dudoladov2016} had been provisionally labelled \texttt{mechanically\_activated}
but its activation mechanism is gallium--indium eutectic infiltration, placing it
unambiguously in \texttt{liquid\_metal\_activated}. No further label changes were
required. Third, five digitized kinetic curves were re-read by an independent pass
using a different screen resolution; mean point deviation was $\approx\!1.5\%$, within
accepted digitization tolerance ($<\!3\%$). Fourth, studies contributing to the kinetic
table were subjected to an Arrhenius quality gate: a linear fit of $\ln(\text{rate})$
vs.\ $T^{-1}$ was required to achieve $R^2\!\ge\!0.90$ on at least three distinct
temperature points. Seventeen studies passed; two were dropped.
\citeauthor{rin2021}~\cite{rin2021} gave $R^2\!=\!0.846$ (one outlier point destabilised
the fit); \citeauthor{ho2016}~\cite{ho2016} could not be fit reliably because the
lowest-temperature reaction was already $\approx\!80\%$ complete within ten seconds and
the additive loading was confounded with temperature across curves. Both retain their
yield rows in the primary dataset but contribute no $E_\mathrm{a}$ estimate.
Fifth, a post-hoc independent-agent inter-rater reliability (IRR) pass
re-extracted the three high-variance categorical labels
(\texttt{system\_class}, \texttt{temperature\_control}, \texttt{measurement\_method})
from a stratified random sample of 19 of the 31 studies
(77 yield rows, spanning all five \texttt{system\_class} levels and all quality tiers)
using fresh agents with no access to the committed codings.
Eight studies were openly accessible; ten were paywalled (Table~\ref{tab:qc_summary}).
Raw inter-rater agreement on the accessible subset was:
Cohen's $\kappa\!=\!0.826$ (\texttt{system\_class}),
$\kappa\!=\!0.429$ (\texttt{temperature\_control}; pre-adjudication),
$\kappa\!=\!0.700$ (\texttt{measurement\_method}; pre-adjudication).
Adjudication against full texts identified three coding corrections:
(1--2) two studies (\citeauthor{trowell2022}~\cite{trowell2022};
\citeauthor{martinezv2026}~\cite{martinezv2026}) re-coded from \texttt{temperature\_control=uncontrolled}
to \texttt{isothermal\_bath}: both papers use an explicit external temperature-control
device (ceramic-fiber heater and PID-controlled heating plate, respectively);
(3) \citeauthor{porciuncula2012}~\cite{porciuncula2012} re-coded from
\texttt{measurement\_method=water\_displacement} to \texttt{other}: hydrogen is measured
by syringe-piston displacement, not a classic inverted-vessel water-displacement apparatus.
Post-adjudication agreement: $\kappa\!=\!0.826$ (\texttt{system\_class}),
$\kappa\!=\!1.00$ (\texttt{temperature\_control}),
$\kappa\!=\!1.00$ (\texttt{measurement\_method}).
These corrections meaningfully changed the \texttt{temperature\_control}
$R^2_C$ from $0.332$ to $0.081$; \texttt{measurement\_method} became the
largest individual predictor ($R^2_C\!=\!0.425$). The headline finding
(methodological covariates explain $\gg$ regime) is unchanged and strengthened
($72\%$ vs.\ $2\%$; was $55\%$ vs.\ $2\%$). The IRR pass is agent-based
(independent agents, same base model), so shared model-level systematic errors
are not fully excluded; this is not equivalent to human dual-coding.

\subsection{Leakage-controlled evaluation}

For each of six comparative regressors (ElasticNet, $k$-nearest neighbours ($k$NN),
random forest (RF), XGBoost (XGB), LightGBM (LGBM), and Gaussian process (GP)) we
estimated the held-out coefficient of determination $R^2$ under two cross-validation
protocols. These six regressors span linear (ElasticNet), kernel (GP), instance-based
($k$NN), bagging (RF), and gradient-boosting (XGB, LGBM) inductive paradigms.
In the \emph{random-split} protocol, records are assigned to folds
independently of their study origin, the standard approach in prior ML literature on
this domain. In the \emph{study-grouped} protocol, all records from a given study are
withheld together, ensuring no study contributes to both training and test partitions.
The \emph{optimism gap}, $R^2_{\text{random}} - R^2_{\text{grouped}}$, measures how
much random splitting overstates cross-study generalization.

Missing values are handled per model family. Tree-based models (RF, XGB, LGBM) exploit
their native missing-value pathways and require no imputation. Linear and kernel models
(ElasticNet, GP) receive median imputation and one-hot encoding for categorical
variables; imputation statistics are computed on the training fold only and applied to
the held-out fold to prevent leakage through imputation. Permutation importance and
SHAP values are estimated from in-sample RF fits and used descriptively.

A negative-control robustness check (TEST~1) substitutes the study-grouped
macro-average $R^2$ with a row-weighted LOSO (leave-one-study-out) $R^2$ to confirm
that the performance collapse is not an artifact of the aggregation rule.

\subsection{Between-study variance decomposition and mixed-effects regression}
\label{sec:variance_decomp}

To identify \emph{what accounts for} between-study disagreement, we aggregate to the study
level (one row per study: mean yield and modal covariate values for that study) and
decompose the total between-study variance of mean yield. For a categorical predictor
$C$ the variance share is
\begin{equation}
R^2_C \;=\; 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SS}_{\text{within}}(C)}{\mathrm{SS}_{\text{total}}},
\label{eq:rsq_c}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{SS}_{\text{within}}(C)$ is the residual sum of squares after fitting
group means of $C$, and $\mathrm{SS}_{\text{total}}$ is the total sum of squares of
between-study mean yield ($n\!=\!31$ studies). We compare the physical-regime label
(\texttt{system\_class}) against five methodological covariates
(\texttt{temperature\_control}, \texttt{measurement\_method}, \texttt{water\_type},
\texttt{quality\_tier}, \texttt{vessel\_type}), individually and jointly.
All headline $R^2_C$ values carry cluster-bootstrap confidence intervals ($B\!=\!1000$,
resampling complete studies with replacement, unstratified across regimes). With $n\!=\!31$
study-level observations and five individual predictors, the effective degrees-of-freedom
ratio is approximately 6:1; the cluster-bootstrap CIs, which resample complete studies,
reflect this limited precision directly. Incremental comparisons and cross-tabulations
use Holm correction throughout.

Complementing the variance decomposition, we fit a mixed-effects meta-regression
(statsmodels \texttt{MixedLM}, REML) with \texttt{system\_class} and standardised
physical covariates as fixed effects, and a random intercept per \texttt{study\_id}.
The mixed-effects model operates on row-level yield data ($n\!=\!299$ rows from
$27$~studies; rows with missing temperature excluded). This directly tests hypothesis
H2: that the physico-chemical regime label moderates hydrogen yield after accounting
for unobserved between-study heterogeneity. Fixed-effect significance uses Wald tests;
all parameter$\times$\texttt{system\_class} interactions use Holm correction. Formally,
the model is
\begin{equation}
y_{ij} = \mu + \sum_{k} \beta_k \mathbf{1}[\text{class}_j = k]
         + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^{\!\top}\mathbf{x}_{ij} + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij},
\quad u_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,\sigma_u^2),\;\varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,\sigma^2),
\label{eq:mixedlm}
\end{equation}
where $y_{ij}$ is the hydrogen yield for row $i$ from study $j$;
$\mathbf{1}[\text{class}_j = k]$ is the regime indicator; $\mathbf{x}_{ij}$ are
standardised physical covariates; $u_j$ is the random study intercept estimated via
REML. The Arrhenius $E_\mathrm{a}$ analysis (H3) applies the same cluster-bootstrap
framework to the 17-study kinetic subsample.

\subsection{Discipline}

The pre-registered family comprises four primary tests: H2 (mixed-effects regime
moderation of yield), H3 (activation-energy regime structure), T\_A (within-study
predictability), and T\_B (between-study variance decomposition). Holm correction is
applied to all secondary interaction tests within H2 ($8$
parameter$\times$\texttt{system\_class} terms).
Hypotheses and success thresholds were pre-specified and frozen before any analysis code was executed
(protocol in \texttt{plan/analysis-protocol.md}, versioned in the companion data release). Language on
observational findings is associational, not causal. Null results are reported as ``no
detectable effect at $n\!\approx\!31$ studies / $3$--$9$ per regime.'' SHAP values and
permutation importances are used descriptively to characterize which features the models
draw on; they are not interpreted as evidence of physical causation. All quantities in
this paper trace to a committed analysis artifact, and the full pipeline is released
with the dataset.

threshold, correction, and outcome; reference this table when assessing whether the
